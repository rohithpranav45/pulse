Industry Overview
The global oil machine: how crude is born, moved, and consumed
Oil is the world's most actively traded commodity, underpinning not just energy markets but the entire architecture of modern industrial civilization. To trade oil macro â€” to hold a view on the direction of the oil price over weeks, months, or years â€” you need a mental model of the entire supply chain, from the wellhead to the car engine. This chapter provides that framework.
The oil industry is conventionally divided into three segments: Upstream (exploration and production), Midstream (transportation, storage, and logistics), and Downstream (refining and product distribution). Understanding where each segment adds value, what its constraints are, and how it responds to price signals is the foundation of all oil market analysis.

Why Understanding the Whole Chain Matters for Market Analysis
A price signal in oil does not simply reflect "supply and demand" in the abstract â€” it reflects the intersection of specific physical constraints at every layer of the supply chain. The 2020 WTI negative price event was not caused by too much crude in the world; it was caused by a specific shortage of storage capacity at Cushing, Oklahoma â€” a midstream problem. The 2022 diesel crisis in Europe was not caused by insufficient global crude â€” it was a downstream problem caused by refinery capacity losses and an embargo on Russian product.
Those who analyze the markets but only watch headline crude production and demand miss the structural levers that set prices. This book is designed to build intuition across the entire chain.
The Integrated Mental Model
Think of the oil market as a pipeline with valves at every junction. Upstream opens or closes the source. Midstream sets the route and speed. Downstream converts the crude into what consumers actually need. A blockage anywhere â€” a strike at a refinery, ice at a terminal, sanctions on a tanker fleet â€” creates a price reaction disproportionate to its physical size.

C H A P T E R   TWO
Upstream â€” Supply Creation
From drill bit to pipeline: who produces oil, how fast, and at what cost
The upstream sector is where oil is found and extracted from the ground. It is the source of price volatility. When those who analyse the markets talk about "supply," they mean upstream. When they debate whether OPEC will cut, whether US shale can grow, or whether a new deepwater discovery changes the long-run price floor â€” all of that is upstream analysis. This chapter builds a complete framework for upstream thinking.
1. Industry Participants
The upstream landscape is a hierarchy of organizations with different cost structures, decision-making timescales, and motivations. Understanding who is producing helps explain why supply responds slowly or quickly to price signals.
National Oil Companies (NOCs)
NOCs are state-owned producers. They control roughly 70% of the world's proven reserves. Saudi Aramco, ADNOC, NIOC, PDVSA, Kuwait Petroleum, and Iraq's INOC are the most systemically important. NOCs operate with mandates that go beyond profit maximization â€” they fund government budgets, maintain employment, and pursue geopolitical objectives.

NOC vs IOC Investment Logic
NOCs do not need to earn a commercial return on equity. This means they can sustain production at prices below the IOC breakeven, and they do not respond to low oil prices by shutting wells. Saudi Aramco's lifting cost is ~$3/bbl; its budget breakeven is ~$70-80/bbl â€” but it will produce whether or not the market price covers fiscal needs. This creates asymmetric supply dynamics: NOC supply is sticky downward.
International Oil Companies (IOCs)
IOCs are publicly listed, shareholder-owned companies: ExxonMobil, Shell, BP, Chevron, TotalEnergies, Equinor. They operate globally, access reserves through concessions and PSAs (production sharing agreements), and must justify capital allocation to investors. This makes them price-sensitive â€” they cut capex when prices fall, and they are the primary drivers of non-OPEC for conventional supply growth.

Independent E&P Companies
Independent E&Ps are pure-play upstream producers, typically US-listed: Pioneer Natural Resources (now part of Exxon), Devon Energy, EOG Resources, Diamondback Energy. They are the heartbeat of US shale production. They are highly responsive to price signals â€” most run short-cycle, low-commitment drilling programmes that can be started or stopped in weeks.
Oilfield Services Companies
OFS companies (Schlumberger/SLB, Halliburton, Baker Hughes, Weatherford) provide the drilling rigs, completion of equipment, seismic surveys, and engineering expertise that NOCs and IOCs deploy. They are a leading indicator of upstream activity â€” when oilfield service day-rates and backlog data rise, it signals that E&P companies are increasing spending. OFS capacity constraints can bottleneck supply responses even when E&P companies want to drill.
2. Supply Types and Elasticity
Not all barrels are equal. The most important dimension for those who analyse the markets is not the absolute cost of oil but its elasticity â€” how quickly supply can respond to a price change. A low-cost barrel that takes five years to bring on-stream is less elasticity-relevant than a higher-cost barrel that can be added in four months.

US Shale / Tight Oil
US tight oil, concentrated in the Permian Basin, Eagle Ford, and Bakken formations, is the defining supply of innovation of the past 15 years. It transformed the US from the world's largest oil importer to a net exporter. The key characteristics that matter for market analysis:
Short lead time: A new shale well can be drilled and completed in 3-6 months, versus 5-7 years for a deepwater project. This makes shale a price-elastic, "swing supply" in the short to medium term.
High decline rates: A shale well typically declines 65-80% in its first year of production. The treadmill problem: operators must continuously drill just to maintain flat production, let alone grow. This creates a capex-intensity floor.
Breakeven sensitivity: The average Permian well breaks even at $40-55/bbl WTI at the corporate level. Below $45, activity falls rapidly. Above $70, the basin grows aggressively. This creates a natural  supply ceiling and floor around those prices.
DUC wells (Drilled but Uncompleted): Wells that have been drilled but not yet hydraulically fractured. They represent latent supply that can be activated rapidly. The DUC inventory is a key leading indicator for near-term supply growth.
The Shale Treadmill Trade
When US rig count drops sharply (as in 2020 or late 2015), shale decline rates mean that production begins falling within 6-9 months even with no further rig reductions. Conversely, a sharp rig count increase signals supply growth 3-6 months forward.
Offshore Conventional
Deepwater and ultra-deepwater projects (Gulf of Mexico, Brazil pre-salt, West Africa, North Sea) have the following characteristics relevant to market analysis:
Long lead times: 5-8 years from FID (Final Investment Decision) to first oil.
Low decline rates once on plateau: Offshore fields decline at 5-15% annually, far lower than shale.
High capital but low operating cost: Once built, the per-barrel operating cost is low. Projects are not shut in below breakeven during temporary price dips.
Concentrated in few basins: Brazil, GOM, West Africa, North Sea. Geopolitical or operational disruptions are localised but impactful.
Oil Sands and Heavy Oil
Canadian oil sands (Alberta) and Venezuelan heavy oil represent some of the world's largest resource bases but are extraordinarily capital-intensive and environmentally contentious. Canadian production (~4.8 mbd) is growing slowly but is primarily committed to existing projects. New oil sands projects are essentially uninvestable in the current ESG/cost environment. Venezuela's production (~0.8 mbd) is a fraction of its historical peak (3.5 mbd in 1998) due to chronic underinvestment and mismanagement.
3. Supply Dynamics: The Capital Cycle
The single most important framework for thinking about medium-term oil supply is the capital cycle. Oil supply does not respond smoothly to price. It responds with long, lumpy lags because the investments required to develop new production are large, risky, and take years to bear fruit. This creates the boom-bust cycle that characterizes oil markets.

FIDs â€” Final Investment Decisions
An FID is the point at which an oil company formally commits capital to a new production project. It is the most important leading indicator of future non-OPEC supply growth, typically with a 4â€“6-year lead time for offshore projects. Global FID activity collapsed in 2015-2016 (following the 2014 price crash) and in 2020 (following COVID). The resulting supply deficit became apparent in 2021-2022 when demand recovered but project pipelines were empty.
FID Drought and the 2021-2022 Supply Deficit
The 2015-2016 capex cuts were the largest in industry history: global upstream investment fell from ~$780bn in 2014 to ~$430bn in 2016. The consequence was felt 4-6 years later: non-OPEC ex-US supply growth stalled in 2021-2022 exactly when post-COVID demand recovered, contributing to the price spike to $130/bbl in March 2022.
4. OPEC and OPEC+
The Organisation of the Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC) was founded in 1960. Its original members were Iraq, Iran, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, and Venezuela. In 2016, OPEC expanded its coordination to include Russia and other non-OPEC producers, forming the "OPEC+" coalition. This alliance represents roughly 48% of global oil production and holds ~80% of proven reserves.

OPEC+ Decision-Making Mechanics
OPEC+ meets formally every 2-6 months (in recent years often virtually). Decisions are made by consensus, though Saudi Arabia holds a de facto veto through its willingness to act unilaterally. The key levers are:
Production quotas: Targets set for each member country. Compliance is monitored by a Joint Ministerial Monitoring Committee (JMMC).
Voluntary additional cuts: Individual members (most often Saudi Arabia) can announce voluntary cuts beyond the quota framework. These are the most powerful short-term signal.
Baseline adjustments: The "reference" production level from which cuts are calculated is politically negotiated and can be gamed â€” members lobby for higher baselines to minimise the actual reduction required.
OPEC+ Compliance and "Paper Cuts"
OPEC+ announcements of production cuts are not the same as actual production cuts. Compliance rates are often poor â€” particularly for Iraq, Kazakhstan, and the UAE, which chronically overproduce. A 1.5 mbd announced cut with 80% compliance is a ~1.2 mbd actual cut. Markets initially react to the announcement, then recalibrate as compliance data emerges from tanker trackers and EIA/IEA monthly reports.
The Saudi Arabia Swing Producer Concept
Saudi Arabia is the only country with the combination of: (a) substantial spare capacity (~2-3 mbd), (b) the fiscal flexibility to operate at variable production levels for extended periods, and (c) the institutional willingness to act as market manager. This makes it the global swing producer.
The concept of a swing producer means that when markets are oversupplied, Saudi Arabia cuts output to drain inventories and support prices. When markets are undersupplied, it increases output from spare capacity. The limitation is that this requires sacrificing market share, which creates fiscal pressure and internal political tension â€” as demonstrated in the 2020 price war with Russia.
5. Spare Capacity
Spare capacity is the volume of production that a producer can bring online within 30 days and sustain for 90 days. It is the most important single measure of the market's ability to absorb supply disruptions. When spare capacity is low, markets are fragile â€” any geopolitical disruption to supply sends prices spiking because there is no buffer. When it is high, markets absorb shocks with modest price responses.

Spare Capacity Quality Matters
Not all spare capacity is equal. Saudi and UAE spare capacity is light/medium sour crude that most complex refineries can process. When the remaining spare capacity is concentrated in heavy sour barrels (as it was in 2007-2008), refineries configured for light sweet crude cannot easily use it. Low headline spare capacity numbers can mask an even tighter effective market for light sweet crude.

C H A P T E R   THREE
Midstream â€” Logistics & Transportation
How crude moves from wellhead to refinery: the plumbing of the oil market
Midstream is the plumbing of the oil market. It receives crude from producers, moves it thousands of miles by pipe, tanker, and rail, holds it in storage terminals, and delivers it to refineries. Those who analyse the markets and understand midstream understand why crude price differentials exist, why prices at different hubs diverge, and why a hurricane in the Gulf of Mexico or ice at a Baltic terminal creates a price spike thousands of miles away.
1. Transportation Infrastructure
Pipelines
Pipelines are the most cost-efficient method of moving crude over land. They are capital-intensive to build, politically complex to route, but once operational carry oil at ~$2-5/bbl compared to ~$8-15/bbl by rail. Pipeline capacity is fixed â€” it cannot be quickly expanded â€” which means that when production in a region grows faster than pipeline capacity, a bottleneck develops and the crude price at the wellhead falls relative to the pipeline exit price.
The Keystone XL saga: Years of regulatory battles delayed pipeline takeaway from Canadian oil sands, causing Western Canadian Select (WCS) crude to trade at discounts of $30-40/bbl to WTI at peak pipeline congestion in 2018.
Permian Basin takeaway: Rapid Permian shale growth in 2017-2018 overwhelmed existing pipeline capacity, causing Permian crude (WTI Midland) to trade at a $20/bbl discount to WTI Cushing.
Russian pipelines: The Druzhba pipeline carries ~1 mbd of Russian crude to Central Europe. The Novorossiysk/CPC system carries ~1.8 mbd of Kazakh crude to the Black Sea.  routes were central to the 2022 sanctions analysis.

The Freight Rate Signal
Tanker freight rates are a leading indicator of oil trade flow changes. When the Houthi campaign closed the Red Sea in late 2023, VLCC and Suezmax rates spiked because voyages rerouted around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10-15 days and absorbing more vessels. Monitoring freight rate moves â€” available from Baltic Exchange, Clarksons, and Fearnleys â€” gives advance warning of flow shifts.
Rail
Rail is the most expensive and capacity-constrained mode of crude transport. In North America, it became critical during the shale boom when pipeline capacity was insufficient. Crude-by-rail (CBR) typically costs $10-20/bbl, making it economic only when pipeline alternatives are unavailable or differentials are wide enough. Rail volumes respond quickly to price signals and serve as a valve when pipeline constraints bite.
Storage
Storage is the interface between supply and demand. When production exceeds consumption, crude goes into storage. When consumption exceeds production, it comes out. Storage is not passive â€” it is actively traded. The economics of storage are central to the contango/backwardation structure of the forward curve (covered in depth in Chapter 8).

Function and Significance
Cushing, Oklahoma (USA)
The delivery point for NYMEX WTI futures. ~85 mmbbl storage capacity. The intersection of ~20 pipelines from Canada, Permian, Bakken, and Midcontinent. When Cushing fills, WTI weakens vs Brent; when it draws, WTI strengthens. The 2020 negative WTI price occurred because Cushing was essentially full.
Rotterdam, Netherlands
Europe's largest oil terminal and refining hub. ARA (Amsterdam-Rotterdam-Antwerp) is the reference point for Brent-linked crude and gasoil/diesel pricing. Rotterdam storage (~50 mmbbl) also serves as European strategic buffer. It is the landing point for much North Sea, West African, and US crude.
Fujairah, UAE
The global bunkering hub and a critical point in Middle East crude logistics. Located outside the Strait of Hormuz, allowing tankers to load without transiting the strait. Increasingly important as a pricing reference for Middle East sour crude and as a floating storage hub during contango markets.
Singapore / Johor
Asia's equivalent of Rotterdam: massive refining and storage hub, pricing reference for Asian products (MOPS â€” Mean of Platts Singapore). Singapore grades VLSFO, HSFO, and gasoil are global bunker benchmarks. Johor Strait floating storage is closely watched for inventory signals.
Hub Inventory Dynamics as Price Signals
Hub inventory data is released weekly (EIA for Cushing) or with a short lag (Euroilstock for ARA, Singapore EDB). Sustained draws at a major hub signal physical tightness and tend to support nearby futures prices. Sustained builds signal oversupply and tend to weaken prices. The relationship is not immediate â€” markets discount 4-6 weeks of expected inventory changes â€” but large unexpected draws or builds move markets intraday.
Cushing 2020: The Anatomy of Negative Oil
In April 2020, WTI front-month futures briefly traded at -$37/bbl. COVID had collapsed demand globally. Cushing storage was at 95%+ capacity. Anyone holding a WTI May futures contract that was going to physically expire needed to find storage â€” and there was none. Rather than pay for delivery, market participants sold the contract at any price to exit, creating the extraordinary negative price. This was not a global oil price of -$37; Brent remained positive at ~$25/bbl. It was a pure Cushing storage bottleneck.

Pipeline Bottlenecks
Pipeline bottlenecks create basis differentials â€” the price gap between two geographically related crude grades. When a pipeline fills up, the crude trapped on the upstream side trades at a discount to the benchmark it would otherwise track. When new pipeline capacity opens, the discount collapses and the upstream crude re-rates to near-benchmark. These events create large, predictable spread opportunities for informed market participants.
Permian Basis Differential Trade
In 2018, WTI Midland (Permian crude at Midland, Texas) traded at a discount of up to $18/bbl to WTI Cushing because pipeline takeaway from the Permian was insufficient. New pipeline capacity (Sunrise, EPIC, Cactus II) came online in stages through 2019, and the basis gradually compressed to ~$1-2/bbl. Those who understood the pipeline schedule captured substantial P&L in the WTI Midland-Cushing spread.
Storage Economics
Storage is only economic when the forward price of oil is high enough to cover the cost of storing it. The full carry cost of storage has three components: (1) financing cost of the oil itself (~$1-2/bbl per month at SOFR + 50bp), (2) physical storage lease cost (~$0.30-0.60/bbl per month for tank), and (3) insurance and inspection costs (~$0.05/bbl per month). Total: ~$1.5-3/bbl per month for land-based storage.
This means that a contango (forward premium) of ~$1.5-3/bbl per month is required to incentivise storage. When contango exceeds full carry cost, storage fills. When it is below full carry, storage empties. This relationship is fundamental to understanding when inventories build or draw and how the forward curve structure relates to physical market tightness.

Downstream â€” The Refining System
Turning crude into value: distillation, cracking, margins, and product markets
The downstream sector converts crude oil into the products that consumers and industry actually use: gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, heating oil, naphtha, and fuel oil. From a macro trading perspective, the downstream is where crude price translates into product price â€” and where the spread between the two (the crack spread) reveals the health and tightness of refining economics globally. Refinery margins are both a driver of crude demand and a leading indicator of product market conditions.
1. How a Refinery Works
A crude oil refinery is an industrial facility that separates and upgrades the hydrocarbon molecules in crude oil into usable products. The complexity of a refinery determines which crudes it can process and which products it produces. This has profound implications for crude pricing â€” a shift in refinery complexity mix changes the global demand for specific crude grades.
Atmospheric Distillation â€” The First Step
Crude oil is heated in a furnace and fed into an atmospheric distillation column (ADU). Different hydrocarbon fractions boil at different temperatures and separate as vapours that condense at different heights in the column. The lighter (lower-boiling) fractions collect at the top; heavier fractions at the bottom.

Secondary Processing â€” Upgrading the Residue
A simple refinery (hydroskimming) stops at atmospheric distillation. A complex refinery uses secondary units to upgrade the heavy residue into higher-value products. This is where refinery complexity â€” measured by the Nelson Complexity Index (NCI) â€” adds value. Higher complexity = higher capital cost, but ability to process cheap heavy sour crude and convert it into premium products.

Nelson Complexity Index
The NCI assigns a complexity weight to each refinery unit as a multiple of basic distillation capacity. A hydroskimming refinery scores ~2-3; a fully complex upgrading refinery with FCC, HCK, and coker scores 10-15+. The global average is approximately 6.7.
Complexity and Crude Appetite
A high-complexity refinery can process cheap heavy sour crude (Arab Heavy, Iraqi Basrah, Venezuelan) and still earn a healthy margin because it converts the heavy residue into premium products. A simple refinery must use light sweet crude to produce acceptable yields. This is why the quality differential (light-heavy spread) expands in refinery-tight markets and contracts when heavy crude is abundant.

The IMO 2020 Sulphur Transition
In January 2020, the IMO introduced a 0.5% global cap on bunker fuel sulphur content (down from 3.5%). This structurally changed the demand profile for high-sulphur fuel oil (HSFO) â€” collapsing it â€” while boosting demand for very low-sulphur fuel oil (VLSFO) and marine gasoil. The Hi-Lo spread (VLSFO-HSFO) became a key trading spread virtually overnight. Refineries with cokers and hydrotreaters that could maximise VLSFO production and minimise HSFO output earned windfall margins.

4. Crack Spreads and Refining Margins
A crack spread is the price difference between crude oil and a refined product, expressed in dollars per barrel. It represents the gross refining margin â€” the revenue a refiner earns per barrel of crude processed before accounting for operating costs. Crack spreads are the most important downstream indicator for oil macro market analysis.

What Wide Cracks Tell You
A very wide gasoil crack (>$30/bbl) signals that diesel/heating oil supply is tight relative to demand. This is often seen in winter, post-refinery outage, or during sanctions on product exporters (e.g., Russia 2022). Wide cracks incentivise refiners to maximise throughput, support crude demand, and attract product imports. They also incentivise shipping crude to the most crack-friendly market (often Asia or Europe depending on the spread differential). Wide cracks are therefore a bullish signal for crude demand.
Refinery Utilisation
Refinery utilisation is the percentage of nameplate capacity actually running. Global utilisation averages ~82-84% in normal conditions. Utilisation drops during scheduled maintenance (turnarounds), unplanned outages, or when margins are so poor that running at full rate is uneconomic. High utilization with tight margins means refiners are being forced to run to meet product demand despite poor economics â€” a sign that the product market is very tight.

Demand Structure
Who burns oil, when, and why â€” the consumption side of the equation
Supply gets most of the attention in oil market commentary, but demand is equally important â€” and in some respects harder to forecast. The IEA, EIA, and OPEC MOMR demand estimates frequently diverge by 0.5-1.5 mbd for the same quarter. Understanding the structure of global oil demand, its drivers, and its seasonality is essential for forming a complete market view.

Road Transportation â€” The Dominant Sector
Road transport (passenger cars and commercial vehicles) consumes roughly 50-55% of all oil. This makes it the most important demand sector. It splits almost equally between:
Gasoline: Dominated by personal vehicles in the United States, China, and emerging markets.   US gasoline consumption alone (~8.7 mbd) is 8.5% of global demand. Highly sensitive to driving season patterns and gasoline prices at the pump.
Diesel: Commercial trucking, agriculture, construction, and rail. Less price-elastic than gasoline because commercial operators have limited substitution options in the near term. Diesel demand is highly correlated with industrial activity and GDP.
The EV Demand Displacement Risk
Battery electric vehicles (BEVs) directly reduce gasoline and diesel demand. The IEA's "Announced Pledges" scenario implies peak oil demand in the late 2020s. In China, BEV penetration has reached ~30% of new car sales (2024), which the CNPC ETRI estimates already displaces ~0.5-0.7 mbd of Chinese gasoline demand vs a counterfactual. By 2030, EV displacement could be 2-3 mbd globally. This is a structural headwind that must be incorporated into long-horizon demand forecasts.
Petrochemicals â€” The Growth Driver
Petrochemical feedstocks are the fastest-growing segment of oil demand. Naphtha, LPG, and ethane are cracked in steam crackers to produce ethylene, propylene, and other building blocks of plastics, synthetic fibres, and resins. Unlike transportation fuels, petrochemicals are extremely difficult to electrify â€” plastics must be made from carbon feedstocks. This makes petrochemical demand structurally resilient even in a world of aggressive electrification.
China is the dominant driver of petrochemical demand growth. Its massive expansion of ethylene and paraxylene capacity through the 2020s absorbed significant naphtha and LPG volumes. Monitoring Chinese patches cracker operating rates is a leading indicator for Asian naphtha demand.
Aviation
Aviation demand (~8 mbd) is uniquely exposed to discrete shocks: pandemics, terrorism incidents, geopolitical crises, and economic recessions all hit air travel disproportionately. COVID-19 reduced aviation demand by ~60% in 2020. The sector recovered to near pre-COVID levels by 2023. Jet fuel is the highest-value distillate â€” it commands a premium to gasoil of $5-15/bbl â€” and is the main product driver for kerosene-maximising refineries in Asia.
Marine Bunkers
The global shipping fleet burns approximately 5 mbd of bunker fuel. Post-IMO 2020, this splits roughly 60% VLSFO (0.5% S), 30% marine gasoil (0.1% S), and 10% HSFO (with scrubbers). LNG-powered vessels are growing slowly. Bunker demand is closely tied to global trade volumes â€” a leading indicator of which is the Baltic Dry Index (BDI).

Refinery Maintenance and Throughput Seasonality
Refineries schedule maintenance (turnarounds) during low-demand shoulder seasons â€” typically March-April and September-October â€” to minimise lost margin. During maintenance, crude runs fall and product output falls. This creates predictable crude demand weakness in March-April and September-October even if end-consumer demand is unchanged. These patterns can be anticipated months in advance.
The Spring Crack Trade
Refineries emerge from spring turnarounds in April-May with fresh catalysts and clean equipment, running at high utilisation just as US driving season demand builds. The combination of rising gasoline demand and full refinery output creates strong crack spread seasonality in March-May. Historically, the March-May period has been the most reliable window for long gasoline crack spreads in North America.

The Inventory System
Stocks as the shock absorber: how inventories transmit supply-demand imbalances into prices
Inventories are the oil market's shock absorber. When production exceeds consumption, inventories rise; when consumption exceeds production, they fall. The direction and rate of inventory change is the most direct, observable signal of whether the oil market is tight or oversupplied â€” and by how much. Sophisticated market analysts monitor inventory data more closely than any other single variable. This chapter explains how the inventory system works, how to interpret it, and how to use it in market analysis.
1. The Global Inventory Structure
Global oil inventories consist of several distinct categories, each with different roles, ownership, and reporting frequencies. The total global stock is estimated at approximately 7-8 billion barrels (including oil in transit and unreported stocks).

OECD Inventories â€” The Market Benchmark
The IEA tracks commercial oil inventories for OECD countries. The aggregate OECD stock level is compared to the 5-year seasonal average to assess whether inventories are above or below "normal." The number that matters is not the absolute level but the deviation from seasonal norms â€” and the direction of change.
The 5-Year Average Framework
The IEA, EIA, and OPEC all reference OECD commercial inventories relative to the 5-year average. When stocks are above the 5-year average, the market is considered oversupplied and price-negative. When below the 5-year average, the market is tight and price-positive. OPEC+ explicitly states its goal is to keep OECD inventories "at or below the 5-year average." During 2022-2023, OECD stocks fell well below this average, providing fundamental support for $90-100/bbl Brent.
US EIA Weekly Data â€” The Market-Moving Release
The US Energy Information Administration (EIA) publishes weekly petroleum data on Wednesdays at 10:30 AM EST. It is the single most market-moving regular data release in oil markets. The report covers:
Crude oil stocks: Cushing and total US. Consensus surveys (Reuters, Bloomberg) poll analysts and market participants for expected changes. A surprise draw (stocks fall more than expected) is bullish; a surprise build is bearish.
Gasoline stocks: Compares to seasonal norms. Low gasoline stocks in May ahead of driving season is a strong positive signal for RBOB futures.
Distillate stocks (diesel + heating oil): Critical in October-November for winter heating season and always for trucking/rail demand.
Crude production estimate: A weekly estimate of US crude output. Closely watched for shale production trends.
Implied demand (product supplied): A proxy for consumption derived from refinery inputs and stock changes.
API vs EIA â€” Don't Confuse the Two
The American Petroleum Institute (API) publishes its own inventory data on Tuesday evenings (the night before EIA). API data is based on voluntary company reporting and is less comprehensive than EIA. However, it is released first and serves as a preview â€” markets often move on the API number on Tuesday evening and then recalibrate on the official EIA Wednesday morning release.
China â€” The Critical Unknown
China is the world's largest crude importer (~11 mbd in 2024) and its inventory situation is the biggest uncertainty in global oil balances. China does not publish reliable commercial inventory data. Analysts estimate Chinese inventory changes by comparing:
Customs import data (crude imports into China)
Independent refinery run estimates (from Kpler, Vortexa, Ursa data)
Tanker tracking of unloading at major Chinese ports
Satellite imagery of floating roof tank shadow lengths at key storage facilities
When Chinese imports significantly exceed estimated refinery runs plus exports, the "missing barrels" are assumed to be going into SPR or strategic storage. This calculation â€” imprecise but important â€” can imply 0.5-1.5 mbd of "invisible" demand that affects global balances.

Floating Storage
Floating storage refers to crude oil held on tankers that are anchored or drifting, neither loading nor discharging. It occurs when:
The forward curve is in deep contango (storage is economic)
Onshore storage is full or unavailable
Buyers want to delay delivery for price reasons
Refineries are running below capacity (COVID 2020)
Floating storage volumes are tracked by Kpler, Vortexa, and TankerTrackers using AIS (Automatic Identification System) vessel positioning data. A sharp rise in floating storage is a strongly bearish signal â€” it means the market is so oversupplied that the excess crude cannot find a home on land. The collapse of floating storage in 2021 as the market tightened was a powerful bullish signal.
The 2020 Floating Storage Surge
In April-May 2020, global floating storage reached an estimated 180-200 mmbbls as COVID demand collapsed and the OPEC+ supply war added 2-3 mbd of unwanted supply. At peak, approximately 200 VLCCs were engaged in floating storage worldwide â€” roughly 400 mmbbls of crude at sea. This overwhelmed onshore storage, triggering the negative WTI futures price. When demand recovered and OPEC+ cut production, floating storage unwound over 6-9 months, acting as a persistent headwind to price recovery.
Strategic Petroleum Reserves
Strategic Petroleum Reserves (SPRs) are government-owned emergency stockpiles designed to buffer against supply disruptions. The US SPR is the largest single reserve at ~360 mmbbls post-2022 releases (peak was ~726 mmbbls in 2010). IEA member countries collectively hold ~1.5 billion barrels across various strategic reserve structures.
The 2022 SPR release (180 mmbbls over 6 months from the US SPR alone) was unprecedented. It effectively added ~1 mbd of supply to a tight market, capping the price rise following the Russia-Ukraine conflict. However, it also left the US SPR at its lowest level since 1984, raising questions about whether it retains sufficient buffer for a genuine supply emergency.
SPR Releases: Temporary not Structural
SPR releases suppress prices temporarily but must eventually be refilled. The US SPR at 360 mmbbls vs a pre-release level of 590 mmbbls means the government needs to buy ~230 mmbbls over the coming years. This is a structural source of demand that is price-contingent â€” the stated buy level for refill is "below $79/bbl WTI." This creates a price floor around that level as a policy backstop.

The Measurement Layer
Data, agencies, and signals: building an informational edge in oil markets
Oil markets are data-intensive. The challenge is not absence of information â€” it is that data is released with lags, subject to revision, published by competing agencies with different methodologies, and often contradictory in the short term. Building a coherent market view requires understanding not just what the data says but how reliable it is, how it will be revised, and what leading indicators are available before official data is published.

EIA STEO as Monthly Anchor
The EIA STEO is the most detailed monthly global balance update available. When the STEO revises global demand upward or supply downward, it often confirms or validates the prior month's price move and provides a framework for the next month's trading. Comparing the STEO to the IEA OMR and OPEC MOMR in the same month reveals where the key analytical disagreements lie â€” those disagreements are often where the trade is.
IEA â€” International Energy Agency
The IEA was founded in 1974 in response to the OPEC oil embargo. It represents OECD countries and focuses on energy security. The IEA's Oil Market Report (OMR), published monthly, is the most widely read global oil market analysis document. It provides:
Global supply and demand balance estimates for current and next year
OECD inventory data and deviation from 5-year averages
Refinery throughput data by region
Commentary on key market developments
The IEA tends to be more demand-optimistic and more willing to forecast oil price declines than OPEC or the EIA. It has been criticised for underestimating Chinese demand in multiple years. Its estimates are treated as credible by institutional investors and government policymakers.
OPEC MOMR â€” Monthly Oil Market Report
OPEC publishes its Monthly Oil Market Report (MOMR) every month, approximately one week after the IEA OMR. The MOMR provides:
OPEC's view of global supply and demand balances
Secondary source production data for OPEC members (Reuters, Argus, Platts)
Non-OPEC supply growth estimates
The "call on OPEC" â€” the implied demand for OPEC production given non-OPEC supply and demand estimates
The "call on OPEC" is critical: if the call is 33 mbd but OPEC is producing 35 mbd, the implied balance is oversupplied and OPEC is building stocks â€” bearish. The OPEC MOMR is often viewed with healthy scepticism because OPEC has an obvious incentive to underestimate non-OPEC supply and overestimate demand â€” both of which justify keeping quotas restrictive.
JODI â€” Joint Organisations Data Initiative
JODI is a collaboration between the IEA, Eurostat, OLADE, APEC, UNSD, and the UN Statistics Division to collect and harmonise oil data from over 100 countries. Its key value is providing data for non-OECD countries that do not report to the IEA individually. JODI data has long publication lags (2-3 months) but is useful for cross-checking IEA and OPEC estimates for developing country production and consumption.

Oil price is not a single number. There are dozens of crude benchmarks, hundreds of product grades, and a futures curve stretching years into the future for each. Understanding how prices are set, how futures markets work, and what the shape of the forward curve reveals about physical market conditions is a core technical skill for oil macro market analysis. This chapter builds that foundation, including four case studies of major historical curve events.

How Benchmarks Are Priced
Crude benchmarks are priced through a combination of futures markets and physical assessed prices. Platts (S&P Global Commodity Insights), Argus Media, and ICIS publish assessed prices based on actual reported trades and bids/offers during defined market-on-close (MOC) assessment windows. These assessments drive physical crude pricing globally â€” cargo invoices reference "Dated Brent" or "Platts Dubai" plus a differential.
Dated Brent vs ICE Brent Futures
ICE Brent futures are financial contracts settling on the ICE Brent Index â€” an average of physical BFOET trades in the assessment period. "Dated Brent" is the Platts physical price assessment for BFOET cargoes loading 10-25 days forward. The spread between Dated Brent and the front-month ICE Brent futures (the "cash-futures EFP") reflects the basis between physical and financial markets. This spread widens when physical demand is strong relative to futures.
2. Forward Curve Structure
The forward curve (sometimes called the futures curve or term structure) is the set of futures prices for delivery of oil at different future dates. It is not a forecast â€” it is the market's current best estimate of fair value for oil at each delivery date, incorporating all available supply/demand information, storage costs, and risk premium.
Backwardation
When near-term prices are higher than longer-dated prices, the curve is in backwardation. This is the normal structure for a commodity with physical scarcity. It reflects:
Current demand for physical delivery is strong (tight market)
Storage is not incentivised (no reward for storing oil because future prices are lower)
Supply disruption risk premium in the near term
Contango
When near-term prices are lower than longer-dated prices, the curve is in contango. This reflects:
Current physical oversupply (buyers can source oil cheaply now)
Storage is incentivised (buy now at spot, sell forward at a premium that exceeds storage costs)
Bearish market sentiment about near-term demand or supply disruption risk

Storage Arbitrage and Contango Limits
Contango cannot be unlimited. The maximum level of contango is bounded by the full cost of carrying oil in storage. When the M1-M12 Brent contango exceeds approximately $15-18/bbl (storage + financing for 11 months), storing a VLCC of oil (2 million barrels) becomes profitable. This brings in physical market participants who buy spot, sell 12 months forward, charter a tanker, and lock in the spread. Their buying of prompt crude and selling of deferred futures caps the contango and brings it back toward full carry.
3. Historical Curve Case Studies
Case Study 1: The 2008 Oil Spike ($147/bbl)
The 2008 oil price spike to $147/bbl in July 2008 was driven by a combination of fundamental supply constraints and speculative excess. The underlying fundamentals were genuine: OPEC spare capacity had fallen to ~1.5 mbd, non-OPEC supply growth was disappointing, and Chinese demand was surging ahead of the Beijing Olympics. The forward curve was in steep backwardation throughout 2007-2008, validating the physical tightness.

In a 4 mbd spare capacity world, it is noise. Spare capacity is the denominator that converts news into price.

Benchmark Relationships

The relationship between crude oil benchmarks is one of the richest areas of oil market analysis. Spreads between Brent and WTI, between light and heavy crude, between sweet and sour grades, and between regional crude benchmarks are all driven by identifiable, tradeable fundamentals. The 2011-2013 Brent-WTI Blow-Out
US tight oil production grew from ~1 mbd in 2008 to ~3 mbd by 2012, all landing at Cushing. But until 2014, US law banned crude oil exports (a legacy of the 1975 Energy Policy and Conservation Act). The Saudi OSP â€” A Market Signal
Saudi Aramco publishes monthly Official Selling Prices (OSPs) for its crude grades to each destination region (Asia, Europe, Americas). The OSP is the price at which Aramco sells to its contract customers, expressed as a differential to the applicable benchmark (e.g., Arab Light to Asia = Dubai/Oman + $X/bbl).
OSP changes are a key market signal. When Aramco raises its OSP to Asia, it means demand for Saudi crude in Asia is strong (it can charge more). When it cuts the OSP, it may be trying to defend market share against competing crudes or responding to weaker demand. An unexpected OSP cut to Europe, for example, signals that Aramco is worried about losing market share to Russian, US, or North African crude.
Monthly OSP as Market Thermometer
Saudi OSP announcements (typically the 5th of each month) are closely watched by physical crude market participants. An OSP that is higher than expected signals Aramco sees tight demand for its crude â€” a bullish signal for Asian crude markets. A cut is bearish. Cross-referencing the OSP with independent assessments of Middle East crude supply/demand gives insight into the physical market conditions that precede futures price moves.

Geopolitics & Risk
Wars, sanctions, chokepoints, and producer alliances: the political economy of oil
Oil is the world's most geopolitically sensitive commodity. Its geographic concentration in politically unstable regions, its role in funding sovereign governments, and its strategic importance to military operations mean that geopolitical events can and regularly do override fundamental supply-demand analysis. This final chapter provides a framework for assessing geopolitical risk in oil markets â€” how to identify what matters, how to size the risk, and how to trade it.
1. Wars, Conflicts, and Supply Disruptions
Historically, the most significant oil price spikes have been associated with supply disruptions caused by conflict in producing regions. The key lesson of oil market history is that geopolitical supply disruptions are most price-impactful when spare capacity is low. With high spare capacity, disruptions are absorbed. With low spare capacity, even modest disruptions cause outsized price moves.

Their impact depends on whether buyers can find alternative sources and whether the sanctioned country can find alternative buyers.

Sanctions vs Embargo: The Rerouting Problem
Sanctions rarely "destroy" supply â€” they reroute it. Russian Urals lost its European customer base in 2022 but found new buyers in India and China at discounted prices. Iranian crude flows to China despite US secondary sanctions. The market impact of sanctions is: (1) the discount the sanctioned country must accept; (2) the cost and delay of rerouting; and (3) the indirect impact on the oil price as Western buyers must source replacement barrels at market price. The net effect is usually smaller than the headline "X mbd sanctioned" figure.
The Price Cap â€” A Novel Sanctions Tool
In December 2022, the G7 introduced a novel sanctions mechanism: a price cap on Russian oil ($60/bbl for crude). The mechanism allowed non-sanctioning countries to continue buying Russian crude, but denied Western shipping, insurance, and financing services to transactions above $60/bbl. This was designed to keep Russian oil flowing (preventing a supply shock) while limiting Russian revenues.
The real-world effectiveness was limited: Russia developed a "shadow fleet" of tankers insured by non-Western providers. By mid-2023, Russian crude was reportedly trading above $60/bbl in many transactions. The experiment showed the limitations of secondary sanctions in a world where alternative logistics and financial infrastructure exists.

Strait of Hormuz â€” The Doomsday Scenario
The Strait of Hormuz is 21 miles wide at its narrowest point and carries approximately 17 mbd of crude oil â€” roughly 17% of global supply and 20% of global crude trade. All Saudi, UAE, Kuwaiti, and most Iraqi and Iranian crude exports pass through Hormuz. The only meaningful bypass options are:
Petroline (Saudi East-West pipeline): 1.5-2 mbd capacity to Yanbu on the Red Sea. Has not been used at full capacity in decades; maintenance status uncertain.
ADNOC Abu Dhabi Crude Oil Pipeline (Habshan-Fujairah): ~1.5 mbd capacity delivering to Fujairah outside Hormuz.
Total bypass capacity: ~3-3.5 mbd vs 17 mbd through Hormuz. A closure would mean ~13-14 mbd of supply disruption â€” an unprecedented shock.
Hormuz Closure: The Unthinkable Trade
A complete Hormuz closure has never occurred and would likely trigger immediate US military response. However, partial disruption â€” Iran mining tanker lanes, IRGC tanker seizures, missile attacks on Saudi terminal at Ras Tanura â€” are lower-probability but non-zero scenarios. In 2019, IRGC seized the British-flagged Stena Impero; in 2023, seized the Suez Rajan. Markets price a risk premium of $3-8/bbl for Hormuz risk at any given time. A confirmed military escalation in the Gulf could add $20-30/bbl within hours.

The Russia Factor
Russia is the world's second-largest crude exporter (~5 mbd) and one of the largest gas exporters. Its integration into OPEC+ since 2016 was a geopolitical as much as an economic decision â€” Russia gained influence over global oil prices while benefiting financially from production restraint.
The 2022 Ukraine invasion fundamentally changed Russia's role in the oil market. Its crude and products were sanctioned by the EU and G7. Its export revenues fell (due to discounts to India and China). Its oil infrastructure came under Ukrainian drone attack. The key remaining uncertainty is the durability of Russian production â€” declining fields, constrained access to Western technology, and limited oilfield services capacity suggest a gradual decline in Russian output over the medium term, even if short-term volumes have been maintained.
Russia Oil Revenue Dependency
Oil and gas revenues represent ~35-45% of Russian federal budget revenues (varying with price). At $60/bbl Urals, Russia runs a fiscal deficit. At $80/bbl Urals, it roughly balances. The EU sanctions and price cap mechanism was explicitly designed to keep Russian oil flowing to global markets (preventing a supply shock) while reducing the price received and therefore the fiscal revenue. Whether it achieved this goal remains debated.

The Products

The previous chapters built a framework for understanding the physical oil market â€” supply, logistics, refining, demand, inventory, and geopolitics.
