Over the internship I designed and built PULSE from the ground up — a complete oil-trading research terminal with a live dashboard. It pulls in ~35 data feeds (prices, forward curves, inventories, positioning, macro and news), learns what each oil spread (the price gap between two futures months) should be worth in the current market, and trades the difference. On top of that sit two more signal engines — one that predicts how prices react to the weekly US oil-inventory report, and one that reads geopolitical news and works out which oil prices it will move — all shown across a 8-tab dashboard and wired into a desk that opens and closes paper trades on its own, all day. Everything below was tested on years of history and reported honestly, net of trading costs.

01 The core strategy — a model for every market

The heart of PULSE estimates the fair value of six oil spreads (Brent & WTI near-month, far-month and butterflies) and trades them back toward fair value when the market drifts too far — mean-reversion, but adapted to conditions instead of one-size-fits-all.

  • 27 market "regimes." I split the market on three axes — the shape of the curve (oversupply vs shortage), inventory level (well-stocked vs tight vs average) and volatility (calm/normal/stressed). Three buckets on each = 27 distinct market conditions, and each spread gets its own model in each one.
  • 7 models compete in every cell. Four linear models (Ridge, Lasso, ElasticNet, Huber) and three gradient-boosted trees (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost) all train on the same data; the one with the best out-of-sample score is picked automatically for that cell — so the method is different where the market behaves differently.
  • What actually wins: across the book CatBoost and Huber are chosen most often — CatBoost is right 67.5% of the time where it wins (Sharpe 1.16), Huber elsewhere for stability.
  • Each model reads 22–24 point-in-time inputs (curve shape, trader positioning, refinery margins, macro/rates, volatility) and outputs a fair value plus a confidence band, so trade size scales with conviction.

05 Predicting the weekly inventory reaction (EIA)

Each Wednesday the US publishes oil-inventory data that moves the market. I built a model that predicts the reaction for crude, gasoline and distillate separately.

  • It measures the surprise vs the real analyst forecast (10+ years of data), pulls the actual number live from the official EIA feed, and previews it with the Tuesday industry estimate.
  • It only calls a trade when it has an edge — a crude surprise is 75–81% predictive in an oversupplied market but a coin-flip when tight, so it stays quiet and points to gasoline instead. I also proved US inventories move WTI ~17× more than Brent.

06 Turning news into trades — AI + retrieval (flagship)

The piece I'm proudest of, and something I hadn't seen done before: a system that reads a geopolitical headline, works out which physical oil asset it's about, and predicts which specific oil prices will move, and in which direction.

  • A map of 37 real assets — shipping chokepoints (Hormuz, Suez), refineries, pipelines and producers, each with its location and how a disruption there moves prices.
  • AI reads the headline. A large language model extracts what happened, where and how serious — and correctly separates "Hormuz closes" from "Hormuz reopens" (opposite trades). A keyword reader backs it up so it never breaks without an AI key.
  • "This looks like…" — retrieval (RAG). For any new headline it finds the most similar past events and shows what each actually did to prices — a transparent, explainable approach, not a black box.
  • A plain-English desk note. The AI writes a 2–3 sentence read grounded strictly in the real retrieved numbers — it is never allowed to invent a figure.
  • A live world map lights up each asset as related news breaks.

08 Risk controls & the live trading engine

  • Never doubles up on the same bet — a correlation filter blocks two trades that are really one risk, and keeps trades that hedge each other.
  • Sizes down when markets get wild — a volatility model (GARCH) shrinks positions as risk rises, cutting the worst drawdown by ~60% and roughly tripling that book's risk-adjusted return.
  • A shock detector spots market stress and pauses new trades automatically.
  • Runs live — reads the desk's 15-minute price feed, re-prices on the current market, logs every signal with its reasoning and confidence, and opens/closes paper trades on its own during market hours.

09 Original ideas I contributed

  • The news-to-price engine itself — connecting a place in the news to the exact tradeable spread it affects is a genuinely new framework.
  • Explainable retrieval (RAG) without a black box — matching events by their real features, so every recommendation can be audited.
  • "Only speak when you have an edge" — used across all three engines: a firm signal when history backs it, otherwise clearly "no edge here."
  • Bridging stale daily data to the live market so the models score on today's prices in real time.

10 How carefully it was tested

  • No hindsight, anywhere — models only ever see past data; results are out-of-sample and after realistic trading costs.
  • Everything is checked — the model's confidence lines up with reality (higher-conviction trades really do work more often), guarded by 284 automated tests.
  • Honesty over polish — every number traces to a named source, stale data is flagged, and results that didn't work are reported plainly.

11 How this fits into a real automated oil-trading desk

PULSE is already built the way an automated (algorithmic) trading desk works — the paper engine becomes a live execution engine simply by swapping the paper broker for a real one. In production it would:

  • Generate signals non-stop from live prices, the inventory calendar and the news wire — the three engines feed one ranked list of trades.
  • Auto-execute with built-in risk controls — volatility-based sizing, a correlation filter against concentration, hard stop-losses, and a shock breaker that halts trading in a crisis.
  • Trade the whole complex — calendar spreads, butterflies, refinery margins (cracks) and grade spreads (WTI–Brent), exactly the products an oil desk runs.
  • Keep a human in the loop — every automated decision comes with its reasoning, confidence and track record, so a trader can supervise or override it.

12 Key lessons & the skills I built

  • A simple, well-built strategy is hard to beat — added complexity only pays in specific, identifiable spots.
  • Fundamentals & geopolitics only matter in the right conditions — inventories in a glut, news in structural spreads.
  • Knowing when NOT to trade is itself an edge — precision beats volume.
